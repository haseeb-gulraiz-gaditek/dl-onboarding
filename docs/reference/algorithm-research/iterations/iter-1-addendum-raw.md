# Iter 1 — Addendum: post-two-tower successors (2023–2025) raw

Iter 1 picked E5/BGE-style dual-encoder as the substrate. This addendum surveys what came after in the LLM era and which of those would actually upgrade that substrate for Mesh.

## Per-paper one-liners

1. **TIGER — Recommender Systems with Generative Retrieval (Rajput et al., NeurIPS 2023, arXiv 2305.05065)** — quantizes each item into a tuple of discrete RQ-VAE codewords ("semantic IDs") and trains a seq2seq transformer to autoregressively generate the next item's codes. Differs from two-tower: no cosine over flat vectors; retrieval = constrained beam search over a learned hierarchical codebook. Gain: 17.3% recall@5 vs SOTA on Amazon, ~20× memory reduction, native cold-start (new items get IDs from content), structurally hierarchical (coarse→fine) in a way flat embeddings are not.
2. **HSTU — Actions Speak Louder than Words (Zhai et al., ICML 2024, arXiv 2402.17152)** — Meta's decoder-only sequential transducer that reformulates ranking+retrieval as one autoregressive generation problem on streaming user-action sequences; new attention/positional design tailored for very long high-cardinality sequences. Differs from two-tower: collapses retriever and ranker into one decoder; no separate user/item encoder. Gain: +12.4% online A/B, 5–15× faster than FlashAttention2 at 8k context, demonstrated power-law scaling up to 1.5T params (the strongest scaling-law evidence for RecSys to date).
3. **OneRec (Kuaishou, arXiv 2502.18965, Feb 2025)** — end-to-end encoder-decoder generative model that replaces the four-stage recall→coarse→fine→rerank cascade and adds DPO-style iterative preference alignment. Differs from two-tower: session-wise generation of an ordered slate; training loop includes preference alignment, not just contrastive InfoNCE. Gain: deployed at 25% QPS on Kuaishou, +1.6% watch-time, opex cut to ~10% of cascade pipeline.
4. **PinFM (Pinterest, arXiv 2507.12704, RecSys 2025)** — 20B-param pretrained transformer over user activity sequences, fine-tuned per surface (Home Feed, Related Items); introduces Deduplicated Cross-Attention Transformer (DCAT) for serving. Differs from two-tower: a single shared user-sequence backbone feeds many ranking heads instead of one tower per task. Gain: +20% engagement on new items, +600% throughput from DCAT, billion-scale serving at neutral cost.
5. **HLLM (ByteDance, arXiv 2409.12740, Sep 2024)** — hierarchical two-LLM stack: an item-LLM compresses each item's text into one embedding, a user-LLM consumes the sequence of those embeddings to predict the next item. Differs from two-tower: encoders are full LLMs (not BERTs), and the user side is autoregressive over LLM-summarized item tokens. Gain: R@5 6.13 vs SASRec 5.14; first-class cold-start because items are described in text, not learned IDs.
6. **Netflix Foundation Model for Personalized Recommendation (Netflix tech blog 2025)** — single foundation model replacing dozens of bespoke task models; shared member+item representations distilled into downstream heads (homepage, search, messaging). Differs from two-tower: not really a retrieval architecture — a representation substrate that *feeds* retrieval/ranking heads. Gain: dramatic engineering simplification, faster transfer of upstream wins, unified personalization signal.
7. **KAR — Open-World Knowledge-Augmented Recommendation (Xi et al., arXiv 2306.10933)** — uses an LLM offline to generate factorized reasoning knowledge about users and factual knowledge about items; a hybrid-expert adapter projects this into the existing CTR model's feature space. Differs from two-tower: keeps the tower architecture; enriches its inputs with LLM-distilled features. Gain: +1–1.5% AUC across 9 CTR backbones; +7% online at Huawei. The cheapest, lowest-risk LLM upgrade path.
8. **RLMRec — Representation Learning with LLMs for Recommendation (Ren et al., WWW 2024, arXiv 2310.15950)** — LLM generates user/item profiles in text; a cross-view contrastive objective aligns the collaborative-filtering ID embedding space with the LLM text-embedding space via mutual-information maximization. Differs from two-tower: doesn't replace the encoder; *regularizes* it with LLM semantics during training. Gain: model-agnostic boost on top of GCN/CF backbones with no inference-time LLM cost.
9. **Does Generative Retrieval Overcome the Limitations of Dense Retrieval? (Zhang, de Rijke et al., arXiv 2509.22116, 2025)** — formal comparison: GR has globally normalized MLE and parameter-encoded corpus, DR has locally normalized contrastive loss and external embeddings. Theoretical edge for GR at scale (no optimization drift, capacity scales with params, not low-rank); empirical reality: **GR does not universally beat DR**. Critical reading; the honest caveat to TIGER hype.
10. **Unifying Generative and Dense Retrieval for Sequential Recommendation (arXiv 2411.18814, 2024)** — proposes hybrid LIGER architecture combining beam-search-decoded semantic IDs with dense-vector reranking, arguing neither paradigm dominates alone. Cold-start and small catalogs favor dense; large catalogs favor generative; hybrids win across the regime.
11. **Investigating Mixture of Experts in Dense Retrieval (Lassance et al., arXiv 2412.11864, 2024) / SB-MoE** — single-block MoE on top of a dense retrieval encoder; shows MoE specifically helps tiny encoders (TinyBERT) but needs more data to pay off on BERT/Contriever-scale. Differs from two-tower: one tower, but with sparse expert routing replacing the FFN layers. Gain: capacity-per-FLOP for small encoders.

## Synthesis — taxonomy of post-two-tower successors

| # | Category | Defining move | Strongest 2024–25 candidate | Mesh fit |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Generative retrieval (semantic IDs)** | Items become sequences of discrete codewords; retrieval = autoregressive decode + beam search over a learned codebook | **TIGER** (NeurIPS 2023) → **OneRec** (2025) for full pipeline | Academically gorgeous. For Mesh's ~300-tool catalog: overkill. RQ-VAE codes need scale (10k+ items) to learn meaningful hierarchies; below that, you're paying training cost for no payoff. |
| 2 | **Decoder-only / autoregressive single-model RecSys** | One transformer ingests user-action sequence, generates next item directly; no separate user/item encoder | **HSTU** (Meta, ICML 2024) | Built for Meta-scale streaming behavior data. Mesh has a one-shot intake form, not a behavior stream — the substrate that makes HSTU shine doesn't exist here. Skip until V2 with usage logs. |
| 3 | **Foundation models for recommendation** | One pretrained backbone over user activity, fine-tuned per surface; shared representation across tasks | **PinFM** (Pinterest 2025) / **Netflix FM** (2025) / **HLLM** (ByteDance 2024) | Right *philosophy* (one shared substrate, many heads), but their value is amortizing across many surfaces. Mesh has one surface. The pattern matters for V3+ if Mesh expands to discovery, alerts, swap-recs, etc. |
| 4 | **LLM-augmented dual encoder (keep the tower, enrich inputs)** | LLM generates features/profiles offline; tower architecture unchanged at inference | **KAR** (Huawei, +7% online) / **RLMRec** (WWW 2024) | **Highest leverage for Mesh today.** Iter 1 already has E5/BGE as the substrate; iter 2 already has LLM intent extraction; KAR/RLMRec are the principled wrapper around exactly this pattern. Composes with everything downstream. |
| 5 | **Hybrid generative+dense + critique** | Don't pick one paradigm; combine, or empirically question generative's claimed dominance | **LIGER** / **Zhang et al. "Does GR overcome DR?"** (2025) | Validation that the iter-1 dual-encoder substrate is *not obsolete* — the headline GR papers haven't won at scale yet. License to keep iter-1 as substrate without apologizing. |

**Just-academic for Mesh:** TIGER/OneRec-style pure generative retrieval (catalog too small), HSTU (no behavior stream), MoE retrievers (no scale need), HLLM (LLM-as-encoder serving cost too high for a single-surface tool).

**Actually upgrades the iter-1 baseline:** KAR-style LLM feature augmentation, RLMRec-style cross-view alignment, foundation-model *thinking* (one shared substrate), and the LIGER-style hybrid mindset (don't bet on a single paradigm).

## Top 3 architectures Mesh should consider for the iter-1 substrate role

### 1. KAR-style LLM-augmented dual encoder (recommended primary)
**What changes:** Keep E5/BGE as the embedding model. Add an offline LLM pass (Sonnet, cached per tool) that generates structured factual knowledge per tool — capabilities, integration partners, friction-points-this-solves, ICP fit, tried-and-bounced reasons. A small adapter (hybrid-expert MLP) projects LLM-distilled vectors into the same space as the text embeddings; tower input becomes `concat(text_emb, adapter(llm_features))`. User side gets the symmetric treatment via iter-2's intent extraction.
**What improves:** Intent-vs-description gap (gap #1 of iter-1) closes structurally — vendor "features" get rewritten into "frictions this solves" by the LLM before embedding. Counterfactual wishes (gap #4) get LLM-tagged as desires, not descriptions. Composes with iter-3 contrastive head, iter-4 cross-encoder rerank, iter-5 ColBERT, iter-6 KG.
**What costs:** One Sonnet call per tool at index-build (~$0.50 for 300 tools, cached). Offline only; serving latency unchanged. No new training loop required; the adapter is supervised on existing labels.

### 2. RLMRec-style cross-view contrastive alignment (recommended for iter-3 retraining)
**What changes:** When iter-3's contrastive head retrains on per-field ColBERT outputs, add a second contrastive loss that pulls the collaborative/structured embedding toward an LLM-generated text-profile embedding for the same tool/user. Mutual-information maximization between the two views; no inference-time LLM call.
**What improves:** Smooths the cold-start manifold (new tools get reasonable positions even with zero feedback) and pushes the embedding space toward semantically coherent neighborhoods rather than co-occurrence-driven ones. The intent-vs-description gap is attacked in *embedding geometry*, not just in features.
**What costs:** Roughly doubles the contrastive training compute. Risk: if LLM profiles are noisy, the regularizer drags the embedding space away from real signal — needs ablation gate before adoption.

### 3. Semantic-ID hybrid (LIGER-style) — keep on the shelf, not now
**What changes:** Train a small RQ-VAE on tool-description embeddings to assign each tool a 3-tuple semantic ID (coarse category → subcategory → instance). Beam-search generation gives a candidate set; iter-1 dense retrieval reranks within that set. Long-term: the RQ-VAE codebook becomes Mesh's hierarchical taxonomy *automatically learned*, not hand-curated.
**What improves:** Hierarchical structure for free, native cold-start (new tools quantize into existing codes), interpretable buckets the concierge LLM can name ("you're in cluster X, here are its instances"). Gives Mesh a real generative-retrieval story for marketing/positioning.
**What costs:** RQ-VAE wants ≥thousands of items to learn meaningful residual codebooks; at 300 tools the codebook collapses to near-trivial buckets. **Defer until catalog crosses ~3k tools.** Until then, the cost-of-capability ratio is wrong vs option 1.

## Verdict for the iter-1 lineage
Iter-1's flat dual encoder is **not obsolete** — the 2025 critique literature confirms generative retrieval has not universally beaten DR, and at Mesh's catalog size DR is the right substrate. The upgrade path is *composition*, not replacement: wrap iter-1 with KAR-style LLM-augmented features (option 1) and RLMRec-style alignment regularization (option 2). Defer pure generative retrieval (TIGER/OneRec/HSTU) to a V2+ where catalog scale and behavior data justify it.
